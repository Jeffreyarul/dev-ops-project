Chapter 2: Literature Review

Comprehensive Analysis of WMSN Security Research

2.1 Historical Development of WMSN Security

The evolution of Wireless Medical Sensor Networks (WMSNs) security research can be traced through several distinct phases, each addressing increasingly sophisticated threats and technological advancements.

Early 2000s: Foundation Period

Initial research focused on basic wireless sensor network security principles adapted for medical applications. Key works by Perrig et al. (2002) on SPINS security protocols laid the groundwork for authenticated communication in resource-constrained environments.

2005-2010: Medical-Specific Security

Research shifted toward healthcare-specific requirements. Studies by Malan et al. (2004) and Gu et al. (2005) addressed patient privacy concerns and real-time data protection in clinical settings.

2010-2015: IoT Integration Era

Integration with broader IoT ecosystems introduced new challenges. Research by Roman et al. (2011) and Sicari et al. (2015) examined security implications of connecting medical sensors to cloud infrastructure.

2015-Present: Advanced Threats

Contemporary research addresses sophisticated cyber attacks, AI-driven threats, and blockchain-based security solutions. Recent works by Alsubait et al. (2020) and Kumar et al. (2021) explore machine learning approaches to intrusion detection.

2.2 Key Research Themes

Cryptographic Solutions

Extensive research on lightweight cryptography for resource-constrained medical devices:

  • ECC-based protocols: Liu et al. (2017) - 40% energy reduction vs RSA
  • Symmetric encryption: AES-128 variants optimized for medical sensors
  • Hybrid approaches: Combining symmetric and asymmetric cryptography
Intrusion Detection Systems

Machine learning and AI-based anomaly detection:

  • Supervised learning: SVM and neural networks for attack classification
  • Unsupervised methods: Clustering algorithms for novel threat detection
  • Edge computing: Distributed IDS for resource optimization

2.3 Research Gaps and Opportunities

Identified Research Gaps
  1. Standardization: Lack of unified security standards for medical IoT devices
  2. Energy-Privacy Tradeoffs: Limited research on balancing security overhead with battery life
  3. Real-world Validation: Most studies lack clinical environment testing
  4. Interoperability: Security challenges in heterogeneous medical device ecosystems
  5. Regulatory Compliance: Gap between technical solutions and healthcare regulations
